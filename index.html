Sixteen months after the 'Zamboanga Siege' – a violent clash between a faction of Moro National Liberation Front and the Philippine government forces in September 2013 killed at least 140 and uprooted 120,000 – almost 20,000 people remain in temporary shelters, struggling to make their ends meet. Assistance for basic needs of the IDPs such as water, protection and education continue while durable solutions are being sought for their long-term housing and livelihood options.

These photos were taken in different evacuation centres and transitional sites for IDPs in Zamboanga between 15 and 27 January 2015. Despite the prolonged hardships, many of the displaced people work hard to help their family and fellow IDPs in the community, while the local government and humanitarian organizations provide framework for better assessing their priority needs and deliver tangible support.

A Grandstand baby. Since the fighting erupted in Zamboanga City in September 2013, # babies were born to families staying in the Joaquin Enriquez Sports Complex, also known as Grandstand. They still do not have a place to call home. [Grandstand]

“I am a mother of four and this is the youngest. The father? He is working as a tricycle driver outside the Grandstand. We are from Santa Barbara (one of the districts worst affected by the siege). Do we want to go back? Of course we want to go back there but we don’t know when we can.” [Grandstand]

“Did you get me?” We find many children in Masepla – probably more than we should. About half of school-age children in Masepla are not attending school. [Masepla-1]

“Our mother is out there to fetch water for us and we are collecting these wires to sell in order to help her.” “We need to make 50 pesos (US$1) everyday so that we can go to school and back.” [Masepla-1] [Credit: Alkhaulan Hashim/OCHA]

“We are here from the Department of Education (DepEd) to screen children – we want to know the exact numbers of those attending school, those not attending school, and those dropped out. Sometimes parents are too embarrassed to say their kids are out of school, or the kids are working. So we have to go door to door, so that we can assess the real needs and concerns for education and help effectively.” According to the DepEd, part of the problem has been conflicting numbers provided by different sources. Two temporary learning spaces recently opened for a growing number of children in Masepla. Findings from this screening will help determine if additional alternative learning space will be needed for IDP children across transitional sites. [Masepla-1]

“Welcome to Masepla-1 transitional site. I come here every day to help the IDPs. My job as a camp manager requires a lot of patience and understanding.” – Camp Manager who works for the City Social Welfare and Development (CSWD) Office. [Masepla-1]

“We are the peacekeepers of Masepla-1. There are five of us, two women and three men. We have day and night shifts.” “What do you do as peacekeepers?” “We patrol the camp, say about 6 times a day, or as needed. Issues we address are mostly litigations between families. Because we’re part of the IDP community, it’s easier for us to understand and talk to them.” “How does being a peacekeeper make you feel?” “Being a peacekeeper in the transitional site is a rewarding opportunity for me as an IDP. The challenges I face everyday are the driving force for me to help maintain peace and order here.” Peacekeepers in the evacuation centres and transitional sites are part of the cash-for-work programme of the Department of Social Welfare and Development (DSWD). [Masepla-1]

“I am an IDP volunteering here to help the nutrition screening of children. We track them, weigh them, and those who are malnourished will get supplementary feeding.” As of February 2015, 41 children suffering severe acute malnutrition and 121 children with moderate acute malnutrition are being monitored and treated across 11 IDP sites in Zamboanga. [Masepla-1]

“We organize psychosocial activities for IDP women and children as well as capacity-building programme for IDP women. Every day, we get groups of little kids together to play. We are also engaging men in some of our activities. After the siege, people were busy rebuilding their own lives and often neglected women and children, who are the most vulnerable group. This women- and children-friendly space provides a neutral venue to hear concerns of these women and children and discuss what we can do to address them.” [At the women- and children-friendly spaces built between Masepla-1 and Masepla-2]

“This tapestry was made by women who participated in our programme – full of wishes for a happy home.” The Integrated Resource Development for Tri-People (IRDT) is a local NGO based in Zamboanga City engaged in peace building and community development work in Mindanao. Here again, some of them are IDP volunteers helping fellow IDP families. [At the women- and children-friendly spaces built between Masepla-1 and Masepla-2]

“I live in Masepla-2 and am also hired as a carpenter here. I am happy because I got a job now. I make 250 pesos ($6) a day and, working 6 days a week, earn 1,500 pesos ($34) a week. My salary as a carpenter allows me to support my family, although it is not enough to send our children to school. My wife and our youngest daughter live here with me, but four of our school-age children are still in Grandstand with relatives during the week because it’s closer to their school. We can’t afford the transportation cost of their commute to the school from here. The siege and its aftermath separated us - I wonder how they’re doing now, if they’re eating alright. But my wife and I want to see them finish their schooling. How I wish every day is Saturday because it is only weekends that my family is complete.” [Masepla-2]

“We were given 5,000 pesos ($113)-worth of goods from aid agencies to open a sari-sari (small retail) store here. For now the store gets us going – we are very grateful for the assistance provided.” [Buggoc TS] [Credit: Mark Bidder/OCHA]

“I build boats. These small ones are for seaweed farming, but I also make bigger boats. I also built Yellow Boats (used by children who otherwise would have to swim to commute to schools). These are sold for 8,500 pesos ($192) each, or 10,000 pesos ($226) with paint finish. If you take good care of them, these boats will last for many years.” “How long does it take to finish one?” “About four days.” “Where are you from?” “I am from Rio Hondo, but was told I wouldn’t be able to go back there.” [Taluksangay TS]

“I volunteer to clean the bathrooms here.” She also makes these strings sold to seaweed farmers for a living. “I’m happy to see foreigners visiting this IDP site, because I can’t go see them where they come from.” [Taluksangay TS]]

“I work for IOM to help camp coordination and camp management at the evacuation centres and transitional sites for IDPs. As part of capacity building, we also train IDPs to find jobs. Today I saw some of our former trainees were applying for jobs at a canning factory – moments like this make me proud of our work”.

“I carried my sick mother to escape from the fire in Santa Barbara during the siege. We’re doing much better here, but what I need now is livelihood support. With 8,000 pesos ($180), I can get a carrying cart and restart my old business of selling isaw (grilled gizzards on skewers).” [PTSI TS]
